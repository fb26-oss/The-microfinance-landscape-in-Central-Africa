 The microfinance landscape in Central Africa (Etat des Lieux du Paysage de la Microfinance dans la CEMAC)

 Overview
This repository hosts a thematic study published by the Bank of Central African States (BEAC), titled Etat des lieux du paysage de la microfinance dans la CEMAC, part of the broader 2019 thematic series on Microfinance et Surliquidite Bancaire dans la CEMAC. The paper was authored by a team of BEAC researchers: Bomba Mbida Frederic, Ndzie Felix, Kenkouo Guy Albert,  and Onomo Betsama Julie. The document was released by BEAC's Direction Generale des Etudes, Finances et Relations Internationales, dated 3 January 2022.
The views expressed in the paper belong solely to its authors and do not necessarily reflect the official position of BEAC.

 Context
Microfinance has long been regarded as a strategic tool for poverty reduction and financial inclusion, both at the community level and at the national level. In the CEMAC region, the sector developed unevenly across member states depending on each country's economic, regulatory, and legal environment. A turning point came in April 2002, when a common regulatory framework for microfinance institutions (etablissements de microfinance, EMF) was adopted across CEMAC, placing every licensed and active EMF under the supervision of the Central African Banking Commission (COBAC). Since then the sector has gradually moved out of informality and has become an increasingly important driver of growth in the sub region.
This study forms part of a wider BEAC research program on the liquidity of financial institutions in CEMAC. It focuses specifically on mapping the microfinance landscape.

 Objective
The paper aims to describe the weight of microfinance within the CEMAC financial ecosystem by presenting the evolution of the sector's main activity and performance indicators, its contribution to financial inclusion, and the nature of the relationship between the banking sector and the microfinance sector. The authors conclude that the relative weight of microfinance remains modest compared with commercial banks but is steadily increasing, while several challenges still need to be addressed for the sector to grow in a stable and sustainable way.

 Structure and Approach
After a brief definition of microfinance, the study proceeds through five main parts:
The regulatory definition of microfinance and of microfinance institutions under CEMAC law.
The evolution of the sector's main activity and performance aggregates, covering deposits, credit extended, treasury position, balance sheet size, contribution to financial inclusion, and profitability indicators, based on COBAC supervisory data for the period 2015 to 2019.
The organization of the sector, covering the supervision framework, authorized activities and services, institutional choices (independent institutions versus network structures), and the typology of EMF categories.
The evolution of the regulatory framework, focusing on the September 2017 CEMAC and COBAC reform and its implementing texts.
The nature of the relationship between banks and microfinance institutions, distinguishing downscaling (banks moving into microfinance) from upscaling (microfinance institutions moving into banking), plus other forms of interaction such as treasury placements and money transfer sub agency arrangements.
The study closes with a set of perspectives and challenges for reinforcing the stability and sustainability of the sector.

 Data Sources
The quantitative analysis relies primarily on supervisory declarations (SESAME reporting) collected by COBAC, complemented by lists of licensed and active EMF transmitted by the monetary authorities of CEMAC member states.

 Key Findings
 Sector size and distribution
At the end of December 2019, CEMAC counted 627 licensed and active microfinance institutions, with Cameroon accounting for the large majority (419), followed by Chad (122), Congo (56), Gabon (18), Central African Republic (10), and Equatorial Guinea (2).

 Activity aggregates, 2015 to 2019
Client deposits fell by about 12 percent over the period, from 944 billion FCFA to 828 billion FCFA, though they rose 17.5 percent compared with the low point recorded at the end of 2018. Cameroon and Congo together account for 88 percent of deposits collected.
Gross credit extended declined by about 3 percent over the period, from 545 billion FCFA to 531 billion FCFA, while non performing loans rose by 32 percent, from 72 billion FCFA to 95 billion FCFA, and now represent about 18 percent of gross credit. The provisioning coverage ratio for non performing loans fell from 81 percent in 2015 to 69 percent in 2019.
The treasury surplus declined by about 12 percent, from 485 billion FCFA to 427 billion FCFA, largely reflecting the exclusion of the Caisse Communautaire d'Afrique from the reporting scope after its transformation into a bank.
Total aggregate balance sheet size increased by 26 percent over the period, from 1 197 billion FCFA to 1 514 billion FCFA, an increase largely attributed to the 2017 regulatory reform, which required the recapitalization of several second category institutions after the minimum capital requirement was raised from 50 million to 300 million FCFA.

 Financial inclusion
Despite remaining much smaller than the banking sector, whose deposits and credit are more than ten times larger, the microfinance sector's contribution to financial inclusion is growing. The number of accounts opened with EMF across CEMAC rose from about 1.75 million in 2015 to about 2.46 million in 2019, an increase of 40 percent. New rules allowing EMF to offer payment accounts and payment services are expected to further strengthen this contribution.

 Profitability
The aggregated net income of reporting EMF reached 4.8 billion FCFA in 2019, up from 2.4 billion FCFA a year earlier, an increase of more than 51 percent, even though the net financial product declined by about 8 percent over the same period. The improvement mainly reflects a reduction in operating expenses, particularly personnel costs and taxes, although the net operating ratio remained high at 96 percent, signaling continued cost pressure in the sector.

 Regulatory evolution
The September 2017 CEMAC and COBAC reform restructured the entire regulatory framework governing microfinance while preserving the original definition of the activity. Key changes include the removal of independent status for first category institutions, which are now reserved for network structures, alignment of legal forms with OHADA rules (cooperative societies for first category institutions, public limited companies for second and third category institutions), higher minimum capital requirements, new governance and internal control standards, new credit granting limits, and dedicated rules for the treatment of institutions in financial difficulty.

 Bank and microfinance relationships
The study identifies two principal channels through which banks and microfinance institutions become connected. Downscaling occurs when a bank moves down market to reach lower income clients, typically through an internal microfinance unit, a dedicated financial subsidiary such as Advans Cameroun, EB ACCION, or the Mutuelles Communautaires de Croissance, or a specialized microfinance service company. Upscaling occurs when a microfinance institution grows large enough to migrate toward banking status, with the Credit Communautaire d'Afrique cited as the sole direct case of an EMF transforming fully into a universal bank in the region. Beyond capital links, banks and EMF also interact through treasury placements and through money transfer arrangements in which EMF act as sub agents of banks.

 Challenges and Perspectives
The authors identify several priorities for reinforcing the sustainability of the sector, including strengthening the framework for recovering non performing loans, reinforcing the capacity and resources of COBAC and national monetary authorities, promoting digital financial services to expand inclusion and formalize transactions currently occurring outside supervised channels, allowing larger EMF and network umbrella structures to hold accounts directly at the central bank, improving the business environment, reducing information asymmetries between promoters and clients, and strengthening financial education among sector participants.
